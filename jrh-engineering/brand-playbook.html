Brand Story

JRH Engineering & Environmental Services was founded on a simple belief: clients deserve engineering that moves at the speed of their projects. Too many firms let work pile up — backlogs grow, plans stall, and permits get delayed. JRH was built to be different.

From raw land to ready-to-build, we handle everything — drainage, utilities, structural design, site development, and environmental services — for developers, homeowners, and public agencies across Texas, North Carolina, and Florida. As a WBE-certified firm, we bring the resources of a large practice with the responsiveness of a firm that genuinely cares about your deadline.

½ logo diameter

✓ Do
  • Use the full-color version on white or light backgrounds
  • Use the reversed white version on the Forest Green brand color
  • Use the monochrome version when full color is not available
  • Always source logo files from the approved asset library

✗ Don't
  • Stretch, skew, or distort the logo in any direction
  • Change any colors within the logo mark
  • Place the logo on a busy photograph without sufficient contrast
  • Add drop shadows, outlines, or effects to the logo
  • Re-create the logo in a different typeface or illustration style

2.4

Iconography

Icons reinforce our clean, modern visual direction. Use a consistent stroke-based icon style — never filled/solid icon sets — to match the open, professional feel of the brand.

Style
Outline / stroke style only. No filled or solid icon sets. Recommended library: Phosphor Icons or Heroicons (both free, outline-first).
Stroke Weight
1.5px–2px stroke weight. Consistent across all icons in a single layout. Scale stroke weight proportionally when resizing.
Color Rules
Default color: Forest Green #1A3A2B. On dark backgrounds: White #FFFFFF. Accent icons (CTAs, alerts): Burnt Orange #B8521A.
Grid & Sizing
Design on 24×24px grid. Display sizes: 16px (inline), 24px (standard UI), 32px (feature cards), 48px (section icons).
Corner Radius
Rounded corners preferred — radius of 2–4px on icon elements to match the brand's approachable but professional personality.
What to Avoid
No 3D icons, no multi-color decorative icons, no clipart-style illustrations. Keep it clean and consistent.
2.5

Imagery & Photography

Photography is one of the fastest ways to signal quality and build trust — especially for a brand revamp targeting larger institutional clients. Every image should feel like it belongs in an Architectural Digest feature on infrastructure, not a generic stock library.

Preferred Subjects
  • Aerial drone shots of completed developments (subdivisions, commercial sites)
  • Civil engineering in action — grading, drainage, site work in progress
  • Blueprint/plan detail shots — hands reviewing drawings in the field
  • Professional team shots — engineers on site, not in a generic office
  • Landmark projects — big builds that establish portfolio credibility
Photographic Style
  • High contrast, clean — mirrors the clean/modern visual direction
  • Warm color grade with a slight green tint to align with brand palette
  • Golden hour / natural light preferred over overcast or flat lighting
  • Wide and establishing shots for hero areas; tight detail shots for service pages
  • People shown confidently, at work — not posed or stiff
✗ Avoid
  • Generic white-collar stock photography (men in suits shaking hands)
  • Low-resolution images — minimum 1200px wide for web, 300 DPI for print
  • Highly saturated, unnatural color grades (Instagram filter look)
  • Empty job sites with no context or sense of scale
  • Images that don't relate to engineering, land development, or the built environment
✓ Sourcing
  • Priority: Real JRH project photos — always more impactful than stock
  • Stock option: Unsplash, Adobe Stock filtered for "civil engineering" or "aerial construction"
  • For proposals & RFPs: Use project-specific photography whenever available
  • Naming convention: jrh-[project-name]-[type]-[number].jpg
03 Part Three

Voice &
Messaging

How JRH sounds in every context — from a LinkedIn post to an RFP cover letter to a client email at 5pm on a Friday.

3.1

Brand Personality

The JRH brand has three defining personality traits. Each trait is paired with its opposite to show the line we don't cross.

Approachable
— Not casual or unprofessional
We talk to clients like partners, not like a distant technical authority. We explain things clearly. We don't hide behind jargon. But we are still professionals who know our field.
Reliable
— Not rigid or inflexible
We do what we say, when we say it. Our brand should feel like a firm handshake — dependable and predictable in the best possible way. Consistency builds trust.
Trustworthy
— Not boastful or overconfident
We let our work and our process speak first. We don't oversell. We share the facts — turnaround times, project scale, certifications — and let clients reach their own conclusion of confidence.
Expert
— Not intimidating or academic
We've worked with counties, cities, and large developers. That experience informs everything. We reference it — not to impress, but to reassure clients they're in capable hands.
Fast
— Not rushed or reckless
Speed is our competitive edge, but it is always paired with precision. We move fast because of our process, not in spite of it. Every piece of copy should hint at urgency and momentum.
Direct
— Not blunt or dismissive
We get to the point. Developers and engineers don't have time for fluff. Our copy is tight, clear, and purposeful — but always respectful and warm in tone.
3.2

Tone of Voice by Channel

The core brand personality stays constant. The tone adjusts based on context.

Channel Tone Example
Website (Wix)
 Confident, clear, conversion-focused. Lead with outcomes. Use short sentences and strong verbs. "From raw land to permit-ready — faster than any firm in Texas."
LinkedIn
 Professional authority with a human edge. Share expertise and project wins. Slightly formal but still warm. "We just wrapped a 47-lot subdivision drainage plan in under 3 weeks. Here's how the team did it."
Facebook
 Community-forward, behind-the-scenes, approachable. Show the team. Tell the story behind the project. "Big week for the JRH team — this project comes from 2 years of relationship-building with the county."
Instagram
 Visual-first, punchy captions. Let the image carry most of the weight. Use minimal text. Bold project claims. "47 lots. 19 days. Done right. ✓ #CivilEngineering #JRH"
Email (Client)
 Direct and responsive. Reference the project specifically. No filler sentences. Always end with a clear next step. "Hi [Name] — attached is the updated grading plan for Lot 12. Changes are marked in red. Let me know if you'd like a call this week."
RFP / Proposals
 Formal, precise, credibility-forward. Lead with differentiators. Use data — turnaround times, project counts, certifications. "JRH Engineering has delivered over [X] civil and structural projects across Texas, North Carolina, and Florida — averaging a [X]-day turnaround on residential permit plans."
Internal Comms
 Casual, collaborative, and team-first. Informality is appropriate. The brand personality applies externally, not here. "Good news on the Harris County project — county approved the drainage plan. Moving to structural next week."
3.3

Key Messages

Every piece of JRH content should reinforce at least one of these five core messages. They are not slogans — they are beliefs that should surface naturally in how we talk about our work.

1
We deliver stamped, permit-ready plans faster than anyone else.
This is the #1 differentiator. Reinforce it with specific turnaround data when possible. "Faster" must always be backed by proof — project timelines, client testimonials, county approval records.
2
We take your project from raw land to ready-to-build.
Full-service positioning. Civil + Structural + Environmental under one roof. Clients don't have to coordinate between multiple firms — JRH handles the whole journey.
3
We communicate — before, during, and after every project.
Directly addresses the #1 frustration clients have with other firms: silence. Reference the kickoff meeting process, mid-project check-ins, and responsiveness as differentiators.
4
We know how Texas, North Carolina, and Florida counties work.
Regulatory familiarity is a silent competitive advantage. Clients working with firms that don't know local jurisdictions face revisions and delays. JRH's county/city experience prevents that.
5
Our best work has never been the loudest thing in the room — that changes now.
Brand revamp message: the quality of JRH's work deserves to be visible. This message is internal-facing (brand strategy) but should inform all external positioning — don't hide the big projects.

3.5

Copy Do's & Don'ts

Real before-and-after examples showing how JRH should and should not communicate.

✓ Do — Website Hero
"Civil, Structural & Environmental Engineering — Delivered Fast, Done Right."
Clear service description + differentiator in one line.
✗ Don't — Website Hero
"Welcome to JRH Engineering & Environmental Services, where we provide a comprehensive suite of engineering solutions to our valued clients."
Generic, passive, says nothing specific. No client benefit. No differentiator.
✓ Do — Email Subject Line
"Updated Grading Plans — [Project Name] · Ready for Review"
Project-specific, action-oriented, instantly scannable.
✗ Don't — Email Subject Line
"FWD: RE: Engineering files attached"
Vague, unprofessional, erodes trust in a firm targeting enterprise clients.
✓ Do — LinkedIn Post
"We completed structural drawings for a 120,000 sq ft industrial facility in 11 business days. The client had been waiting 8 weeks with their previous firm. Turnaround matters."
Specific numbers, clear contrast with the competition, no bragging — just facts.
✗ Don't — LinkedIn Post
"Excited to share that JRH Engineering is the best engineering firm in Texas! We work hard for our clients every day! #Engineering #JRH #Blessed"
Exclamation spam, unsupported claims, hashtag overload. Reads like a small business, not an enterprise firm.
✓ Do — RFP Opening
"JRH Engineering has delivered over [X] permit-ready civil and structural plans across Texas, North Carolina, and Florida — averaging a [X]-day turnaround on residential projects. We are WBE-certified and have direct experience working with [County Name] on drainage and utilities approvals."
Data-led, credential-specific, shows local knowledge immediately.
✗ Don't — RFP Opening
"JRH Engineering is a full-service engineering firm committed to excellence and client satisfaction. We look forward to the opportunity to serve your project needs."
Zero specifics. Could be written by any firm in any industry. Decision-makers discard proposals that open this way.

Email Copy Rules
  • Subject line: Project name first, then action needed. Max 50 characters.
  • Greeting: "Hi [First Name]" — no "Dear Sir/Madam" ever.
  • Opening line: Reference the project or last conversation immediately.
  • Body: Max 3 short paragraphs. Use bullet points for deliverable lists.
  • Closing: Always include a specific next step or question.
  • Sign-off: "Best," or "Thanks," — not "Regards," or "Sincerely,"
  • Attachments: Name files as JRH-[ProjectName]-[Type]-v[#].pdf
Website (Wix) Copy Guidelines
Hero Section
  • Headline: 8 words max. Outcome-first.
  • Subheadline: 1–2 sentences, no jargon.
  • CTA button: "Get a Free Consultation" or "View Our Work"
  • CTA color: Burnt Orange #B8521A
Service Pages
  • Open with the client problem, not the service description.
  • Use H2 for service name, H3 for sub-services.
  • Include at least one stat or turnaround time claim.
  • End every service page with a clear CTA.
General Rules
  • Use "you" and "your" — speak to the visitor directly.
  • Active voice only: "We deliver" not "Plans are delivered by us."
  • Contractions are fine on web: "We'll" "You're" "Don't"
  • Avoid: "solutions," "leverage," "synergy," "world-class"
